Understanding the employee/employer relationship is important before we begin learning about the compensation mix options.

In Canada, the CRA articulates the determination of this relationship. It is important for companies and their management to understand the difference between an employee/employer relationship compared to a contractor/organization relationship.

[[style:request]]

Creator+ jumbo callout

The difference is determined by the intention of the relationship. Is it a contract of service or a contract for service? A contract of service is an employee relationship. A contract for service is a business relationship.

[[/style]]

A contract of service is formalized through the acceptance of a position with a company by an individual. Terms and conditions of employment are set-out and agreed to by both parties. Alternatively, with a contract for service, the organization is setting out expectations for the delivery of specified services with another company or with an individual.

Organizational compensation systems can include any combination of these three fundamental compensation components (Base, Performance, and Indirect Pay). For example, a company may compensate employees with base pay plus only the mandatory benefits required. Or perhaps the company compensates purely on commissions (performance pay) along with the required mandatory benefits. Many large organizations will have a compensation system that includes all three components.

Compensation Mix Choices

Your compensation mix choices are:

Base Pay

  • Job evaluation

  • Market pricing

  • Pay for knowledge

Performance Pay

  • Individual performance

  • Group performance

  • Organization performance

Indirect Pay

  • Mandatory benefits

  • Pension plan

  • Health and life insurance

  • Pay for time not worked

  • Employee services

  • Other benefits

But then the question is, what percentage of total compensation should each component represent? Should base pay be 80% of total compensation with 10% performance and 10% indirect? These questions become more complex if the organization considers different compensation options for different job categories (job families).

Consider a car dealership as an example. Here, different categories of positions receive different compensation mix rewards. Study this table:

Job Category Base Pay Performance Pay Indirect Pay
Administration 90%   10%
Sales 40% 50% Individual 10%
Management 60% 25% 15%

Given the number of options available, how do companies determine the optimal compensation mix? The answer to this question comes back to the purpose of the compensation system – to help create a willingness among qualified persons to join the organization & to perform the tasks needed by the organization.

4.2.0: Discuss Base Pay

[[style:calloutRight]]

Base pay represents compensation for time worked. It is differentiated between a wage or a salary, with a wage representing a dollar value per hour, versus a salary which is represented as a weekly, monthly, or annual amount.

[[/style]]

“Base pay tends to reflect the value of the work or skills and generally ignores differences attributable to individual employees” (Milkovich, et al., 2017, p. 6). For example, a company will have a base pay for the position of seamstress of $20/per hour. Employees working as a seamstress may begin at this base wage and then receive increases to their base pay through merit pay increases, or cost-of-living adjustment (COLA), or regular increments based on years of service as outlined in a collective bargaining agreement or employment contract.

4.2.3 Common Base Pay Methods

Determining if and to what extent base pay will be leveraged, is only one decision that needs to be made by organizations. The method to determine the amount of base pay for each job still needs to be established and implemented.

There are three common base pay methods: Job Evaluation, Market Pricing, and Pay for Knowledge. There are many factors that are relevant in determining how to determine base pay. It is common for companies to use more than one of these methods in the determination of the amount of base pay to pay their employees.

4.3.0: Explain Performance Pay

[[style:calloutRight]]

Performance pay is “any type of financial reward provided only when certain specified performance results occur (Long & Singh, 2018, pg.103).

[[/style]]

Generally, base pay in Canada represents 75% to 80% of total compensation while performance pay represents approximately 5 to 10% of total compensation (Long & Singh, 2018). The ratio of performance pay is highly dependent on the industry. For example, car sales, consulting or financial advising may include commissions in excess of 50% of total compensation.

Performance pay which is based on some measure of performance, is a shift from the entitlement aspect of base pay.  Recall that with base pay, if an employee attends work and performs well enough to maintain employment, then they are entitled to be paid the same as their peers.  With performance pay, a company seeks to encourage and motivate employees to some pre-established outcomes or measures.  Such performance and respective performance pay can be related to individual, group or organizational performance. 4.3.2 Advantages and Disadvantages of Performance Pay

Performance pay have some advantages and disadvantages.

Advantages

Let’s consider why an organization may want to include performance pay in its compensation system. Performance Pay plans can:

  • Motivate employees to perform specific behaviours or meet desired goals;

  • Minimize the need for mechanisms or processes to control employee behaviour as their pay is dependent on performing desired behaviours;

  • Engage employees in understanding current performance and performance that leads to higher pay;

  • Align well with the organization’s ability to pay its employees because performance pay is variable. Unlike base pay, performance pay is more flexible.

  • Be leveraged as a mechanism to support specific managerial strategies.

[[style:note]]

Use Dotted Underline with Tooltip for the following.

Assumes most employees work to satisfy their social needs and that employees inherently dislike their work.

[[/style]]

For example, group-based options can support both human relations and high-involvement strategies. Individual performance pay can support the classical managerial strategy. Group and organization plans can support the high-involvement strategy.

Disadvantages

Given the advantages stated above, why are performance plans not used by all companies or to a greater extent (beyond 5 to 10% of total compensation)? Perhaps because there are some disadvantages:

  1. While performance pay aligns with an organization’s ability to pay, employees tend to prefer the certainty of base pay over the risk of compensation based on performance.

  2. When performance pay is a bonus above base pay, or is a small portion of total compensation, then acceptability among employees does exist.

  3. If the performance measures are set by management without consultation of employees and especially if they are aggressive targets, employees will be unable to achieve the targets and can actually be de-motivated by the performance pay option.

  4. Conflict and mistrust can become problematic between employees and management.

  5. One of the biggest drawbacks of performance pay is the limitation to one or a few behaviours while other important desired behaviours are ignored.

4.4.3 Categories of Benefits

Let’s begin by identifying and classifying the different types of employee benefits. It is important to understand that there are both mandatory and voluntary benefits that form the array of employee benefit offerings.

Mandatory Benefits

Recall at the beginning of this learning outcome, we said that it is not possible for a company to have 100% of their compensation costs be attributable to base pay alone. The reason is due to the requirement for all Canadian companies to administer and pay premiums for three mandatory employee benefits:

  1. Employment Insurance (EI)

  2. Canada Pension Plan (CPP)

  3. Workers’ Compensation (WCB)

Employees often don’t associate these programs as employee benefits but consider why they exist: to provide safety and financial security in the case of a workplace accident, possibility of job loss and future source of income upon retirement.

4.4.5 Contributory and Non-Contributory Plans

[[style:note]]

For the following, create Dotted Underline with Tooltip:

Costs are shared between the employer and the employee.

[[/style]]

It is important to understand who is responsible for the cost of these benefits. In the case of EI and CPP, both the employer and the employee pay premiums to the programs. This is referred to a contributory plan. WCB premiums, on the other hand, are paid for entirely by the employer. This is referred to as a non-contributory plan. 

While these mandatory benefits are not important from a recruitment or retention perspective, every company with employees, must factor the costs of the premiums into the total compensation costs. Due to these mandatory benefit costs, companies may consider if alternative employment relationships such as contracted services should be incorporated into operations. Such a decision will impact other structural variables such as job design, control and decision-making.

There is one more employee benefit that is legislatively required - vacation. The Canada Labour Code establishes the requirements for two weeks of vacation leave or vacation pay after completing a year of employment. Provinces can improve upon this legislated benefit. In Saskatchewan, The Saskatchewan Employment Act provides for a minimum of 3 weeks of vacation leave after one year of employment.

Employees that work full-time hours will be provided paid time off for vacation based on the jurisdiction they work in or, in some cases, the jurisdiction of their employer. Employees that work less than full-time hours will receive pro-rated paid time off or the employer may pay the benefit out to employees each pay period.

Similar to the mandatory benefits, employers must ensure that they consider the cost of this paid-time-off benefit in their workforce planning and payroll budgeting.

4.4.9 Pros and Cons of Indirect Compensation

Indirect compensation can be costly and given the lack of flexibility in modifying or eliminating them, organizations need to complete comprehensive analysis including a cost/benefit analysis. Such employee benefit programs can support:

  • Attraction

  • Retention

  • Motivation

However, they can also create an entitlement mentality among employees given there is no direct correlation to employee performance.

Throughout this learning outcome, the different components of the compensation mix were outlined. There is no one correct ratio of base, performance, and indirect pay that an organization will employ. Instead, each organization will strive to align the mix with its strategic goals and the key behaviours that it requires of its employees.

To solidify your learning of the concepts presented in this learning outcome, you will assess your knowledge in a quiz that is combined with LO5 and LO6 (Quiz #2).
